## The Small Ceremony of Choosing

The art of a handwritten Christmas card begins before the pen touches paper. It begins with the sorting: the cream envelopes, the slightly stiff cards, the one with the robin you bought because it looked like your grandmother’s hallway. You make tea you may forget to drink. The kitchen lamp throws a gold square over the table, and the window goes black enough to become a mirror.

There is a pleasure in choosing the right card for each person. Not the grandest one. The truest one. A snowy lane for the friend who still walks her dog before dawn. A brass bell for the aunt who keeps tangerines in a blue bowl. A plain linen card for the neighbor who shovels without being asked.

> A handwritten Christmas card is not a task to finish; it is a small room you open for someone else to enter.

This is where the ritual earns its keep. In a season full of announcements, the card asks for nothing more than presence. It says: I held you in mind while the house was quiet.

## A Few Lines That Sound Like You

The note need not be polished. In fact, polish can be a little suspicious. Christmas ink should smell faintly of the room it was written in: cinnamon, wool, woodsmoke, or the clean citrus of a freshly peeled orange. It should carry the evidence of a human hand — the crossed-out word, the tiny wobble, the heart dotting the i because you were eight years old when you learned that trick and never quite outgrew it.

Start simply. Dear Mara, I loved hearing your laugh on the phone last week. Dear Tom, the garden photographs made this gray afternoon brighter. Dear Mrs. Alvarez, your mince pies were a mercy.

Specificity is the candle in the window. It is better to write three honest lines than twenty borrowed ones. Mention the book they lent you, the soup they brought, the way their child sang carols off-key with magnificent confidence. If the year has been hard, you may keep it tender rather than bright: I have been thinking of you often, and I am grateful you are in my life.

Then sign your name fully. There is intimacy in a full signature. It belongs to the card as much as the stamp.

## The Walk to the Postbox

When the stack is done, tie it with twine or slip it into a basket. Press the envelopes flat beneath a book for an hour if you like ceremony; let them lean in a crooked pile if you prefer life. Either way, they become a little weather system on your table — names and promises, ink still drying, futures travelling.

The final act is the walk to the postbox. Go slowly if you can. Winter air will sharpen your cheeks. Your boots will sound on the pavement, or crunch if you are lucky enough to have snow. Somewhere a dog barks. Somewhere a curtain shifts. Somewhere, in a house not unlike yours, someone is setting out a plate for biscuits and wondering who might remember them.

By the time you reach the box, the cards are no longer only yours. They have become small vessels of continuity: paper against paper, memory against distance, one hand reaching another through the cold.

And perhaps that is the old magic of them. Not perfection. Not speed. Not even news.

Just the quiet proof that, before the year turned, you took a little time to be known.
