There's a moment, right around the time the oven hits 350 degrees and the molasses meets the flour, when the whole house starts to smell like December. Not the idea of December — the real thing. The warm, spiced, slightly burnt-at-the-edges version that no candle has ever been able to replicate.

## The Old-World Recipe

Real gingerbread starts with quality molasses — not the light, syrupy kind, but the dark, robust blackstrap variety that tastes like it has a story to tell. Combine it with freshly ground ginger, ceylon cinnamon, and a pinch of clove, and you have a dough that could have come from any century.

The secret most modern recipes skip: let the dough rest overnight in the cold. This isn't just tradition for tradition's sake — the resting period allows the spices to bloom and the gluten to relax, giving you a dough that rolls cleanly and bakes into walls sturdy enough to hold a roof of royal icing.

## Building the House

Construction is half engineering, half patience. Start with the walls, let them cool completely on the pan, and use a thick royal icing as your mortar. The number one mistake first-timers make is rushing — if the icing hasn't set for at least twenty minutes, your walls will slide like a house on a hillside.

> A gingerbread house is the only architecture where the building inspector is a five-year-old with sticky fingers.

Decorate with what feels right: peppermint rounds for stepping stones, pretzel sticks for a fence, powdered sugar dusted through a fine sieve for that first-snow-of-the-season look.

## Making It Your Own

The best gingerbread traditions are the imperfect ones. The lopsided chimney, the icing that pooled in the wrong corner, the gumdrop that someone definitely already licked. These are the details that make a gingerbread house a memory, not a showpiece.
