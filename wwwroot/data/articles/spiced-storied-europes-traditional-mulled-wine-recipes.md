You kick the snow off your boots, the chill of the evening still biting at your ears, before the front door swings open to a wall of warmth—and that scent. Clove, orange peel, a sharp tang of red wine simmering low on the stove. It is the scent of winter across Europe, though no two pots are quite the same.

## Nordic Glögg: Sweet, Spiced, Storied

In the Nordic countries, glögg is less a beverage than a midwinter ritual. You’ll find it simmered with cardamom—that bright, piney note that cuts through the dark, heavy red wine—plus raisins and blanched almonds floated on top, a texture surprise against the smooth liquid. Old recipes call for a splash of aquavit, too, the sharp spirit cutting the sweetness, though many modern home cooks skip it for a gentler sip. It is served steaming at julmarknad stalls, your mittens tossed on the wooden counter as you cup the warm mug between your palms.

> "A pot of glögg left to simmer all afternoon is the only proper welcome for a winter guest, for it carries the heat of the hearth and the kindness of the host." — 1892 Swedish Household Manual

## Alpine Glühwein: Sharp, Citrus, Bright

Cross the Alps to Bavaria or Tyrol, and glühwein takes a sharper, citrus-forward turn. Here, the wine is often a dry, tart red—nothing too fruity—simmered with whole cinnamon sticks, star anise, and thick slices of unwaxed orange that release their oils as they steep. Many stalls at Munich’s Christkindlmarkt will offer a shot of rum stirred in, or a sugar-dusted rim that crunches against your lip as you sip. It is a drink for wandering, for pausing under strings of fairy lights as snow dusts your scarf, the faint jingle of market bells drifting on the cold air.

## Mediterranean Vin Brulé: Herbal, Light, Unexpected

In Italy’s Piedmont region, vin brulé leans herbal, not just sweet. Rosemary sprigs and lemon zest join the usual clove and cinnamon, the resinous herb cutting through the rich Barbera wine used there. In France’s Alsace region, vin chaud often adds a strip of lemon peel and a pinch of nutmeg, lighter than its Alpine neighbor, sipped slowly as you wander Strasbourg’s cobblestone lanes. Spanish ponche de vino adds a cinnamon stick and a dash of brandy, served in small glass cups at twilight family gatherings.

We lose something when we buy pre-mixed mulled wine sachets, I think—the act of measuring the spices yourself, of smelling the cardamom as you crush it, of adjusting the heat to your liking, is as much a part of the tradition as the drinking.

There is a particular kind of magic in a pot of simmering wine, the way it fills a room with scent, the way it draws people to the kitchen to lean against the counter and chat. It is not just a drink. It is a thread, connecting you to centuries of winter evenings, to strangers in far-off market stalls, to the simple, unchanging comfort of warmth in the cold.
