### Gathering the Echoes of Winter
Walking through the faded display of the local grocery store’s Christmas aisle, I found a withered sprig of dried rosemary behind a Hallmark card — its needles brittle but still red, still whispering of evergreen forests. This, I realized, was my raw material. Christmas potpourri isn’t just about scent; it’s about unearthing the quiet traditions buried in dried jars and forgotten recipe tins. My grandmother’s kitchen once overflowed with clove-studded oranges and cinnamon-dusted blankets. Now, I dust off my own mortar and pestle, gathering fragments of a language spoken in spices and woodsmoke. 

#### The Ritual of Repurposing
There’s a poetry in repurposing — turning spindly pine branches clipped from a holiday tree into fragrant pomanders, or threading dried citrus wheels onto twine to hang by doorways. I save tea leaves from afternoon cups, layer lavender buds collected from a dusty greenhouse, and toast almonds salvaged from forgotten charcuterie boards. Each ingredient carries a story: the rosemary that survived a winter gale, the oranges that bloomed in a sun-drenched windowsill. Combining them in a earthenware bowl, I shake gently, letting the oils mingle into a tapestry of bergamot and pine. 

#### A Language of Scented Moments
When the mixture dries, I transfer it to a porcelain dish nestled in the knee hollow of a rocking chair. The scent becomes a flag of memory — sharp and citrusy at first, then softening into something warm, like the ache of a hearth. If I squeeze a few cloves into my palm and crush them, the crispness knocks echoes from my childhood. Sometimes I add a piece of dried parchment tucked inside the potpourri: handwritten in looping ink, a note to “carry this warmth into the new year.” Potpourri is not just decoration; it’s a suspended gesture, turning transience into ritual, ordinary scraps into relics. Here, in the quiet before a storm, I revisit a harvest long past.
