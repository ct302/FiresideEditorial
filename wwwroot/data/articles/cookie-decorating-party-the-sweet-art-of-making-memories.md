Craft & Care
---
Have you ever stood in a kitchen, surrounded by half-empty bowls of powdered sugar and a mountain of glittering sprinkles, wondering if anyone else is as lost as you? The kind of moment that could’ve been a disaster if it weren’t for the invisible glue of joy your friends poured into hauling out cookie sheets and squishing icing together.

I learned the hard way: You can only guide, never choose. Forcing the perfect decorator onto a stranger’s hands will absolutely break. Instead, leave the detailed icing patterns to the seasoned bakers and the ‘fancy’ sprinkles to the crafty chaos gremlins. This is a party for experimentation, not precision. As the 14-year-old fairy-turned-modern-day-maestro pointed out in the first round of gingerbread men colonization, “Bad shape is just art we haven’t discovered yet.”

The room should simmer at a balance of warmth—neither the cold sippy-cup brigade nor the stove-dough-roasting inferno. I recommend layering a honey-scented tea tray at the center so guests can sip away the chaos, and if there’s a modicum of meat thermometer at hand, set the oven to 350°F before you open that bag of royal icing.

One quirk: Always keep one person on the receiving end of lollipop domination. That girl—her hair sashaying, her eyes shining like a disco ball—will inevitably take over the “unicorn maintenance” duties, and you won’t understand it until you are. Admire the magic of her winged tails and chromatic hooves while the rest of us go through the motions, scribbling naked cookies like peasant farmers.

Tell stories. Not words—PET TALK. The observation that the person who volunteers for blue-centered cookies is always the one who’ll end up with hers frozen on the back of the refrigerator is a competitive point between baker clans. The secret of hiding salt in the frosting is a clan-versary. The fact that the person you’re sitting next to finished the third pair of icing swabs before you even undid their still-open toothpaste roller screen essay is WIN-WIN.

Wind down by pairing the “winning” decorated cookies with a pot of cocoa that tastes vaguely of a pep rally and lots of chaos. Let hands stretch out, samples break, and giggles echo. The real recipe isn’t in the mix—though the sugar does help. It’s in the roster: friends who matter more than cookie perfection, and a kitchen hot enough to comfort their laughter.

**Key takeaway:** No cookie will ever be ruined if you’re smiling while you decorate it.

*The end of this messy, sticky evening isn’t a wall.* It’s just the graphite before the next drawing.
