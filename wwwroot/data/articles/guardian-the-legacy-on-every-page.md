## Crafting a Recipe Legacy
There’s a reason Grandma’s card never got lost in the drawer. We turned it into a ritual. Every Christmas Eve, we’d sit at the old kitchen table, her worn silver fork resting beside me as she read each step aloud. The sound of her voice, the smell of cinnamon curling from the bowl—those elements made the recipe *alive*. My sister tried Googling ‘plum pie crust’ once. The recipe she found was fast and precise, but it lacked that *weighted* silence we associated with favor. The future doesn’t inherit what we digitize. It inherits what we *embody*. 

## The Flavor of Remembering
Let me be clear: I’m not anti-digital. My tablet holds a spreadsheet of spice conversions, safe from Grandma’s handwritten notes getting damp. But the digital version can’t replicate the moment my grandmother pressed her damp apron to the parchment, claiming, *‘This is how you *feel* when you make it right*.’ A recipe is a story waiting to be tasted. When my niece asked last year for the ‘easy’ version, I hesitated—until I handed her the card with her great-grandmother’s facewriting in the margin: *‘Use your own butter, even if it’s not perfect. Perfection isn’t the point here.’* Her eyes stung. That’s when I knew: recipes aren’t recipes if they’re not shared. They’re letters. They’re heirlooms. They’re the kind of backwardness that keeps time from erasing everything.

Recipes fade with us. But they don’t have to. We just have to teach them how to live beyond us.
