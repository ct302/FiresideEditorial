# A Cookie Decorating Party, Sweetly Set

There is a particular hush before a cookie decorating party begins. The kitchen glows warmer than the rest of the house. Bowls of sprinkles sit like tiny stained-glass windows. Royal icing waits in piping bags, smooth and glossy as candle wax. Somewhere, a pot of cocoa murmurs on the stove, and everyone arrives with flour on their cuffs before they have even touched a cookie.

This is not a party that needs to be polished. It is a party that asks for aprons, patience, and a table large enough for elbows. The joy is in the making — in the smudges, the crooked trees, the snowmen with three arms, the star that somehow looks like a comet. Children will work with fierce concentration. Adults will pretend they are not competing. Everyone will leave with a tin that smells faintly of vanilla and winter.

## Set the Table Before the Guests Arrive

Lay out your supplies in small, manageable piles. A few colors of icing are better than a rainbow that overwhelms the room. Choose two or three shades — cream, berry red, pine green — and let the sprinkles do the sparkling. Little ramekins are perfect for nonpareils, sanding sugar, crushed peppermints, and cinnamon hearts.

Put waxed paper or parchment beneath each place setting. It makes cleanup merciful, and it gives every guest a soft white field for their edible art. Keep damp cloths nearby, because sticky fingers are part of the ritual. If children are coming, consider setting one station for piping and another for decorating with spoons and toothpicks. Less fuss, more wonder.

## Keep the Icing Simple, the Atmosphere Warm

The secret to a relaxed cookie party is not flawless frosting. It is a host who has already forgiven the mess.

> A good decorating table does not demand perfection. It invites a little sweetness to gather.

Make the cookies ahead, when the house is quiet and the baking smells can seep into curtains and wool sweaters. A sturdy sugar cookie, cooled completely, is the best canvas. Royal icing dries smooth, but a simple powdered-sugar glaze is gentler if you want something quick. Either way, keep the cookies flat and firm enough to travel home in paper plates or tins.

Warm drinks matter. Cocoa with marshmallows, cider with a cinnamon stick, or tea in mismatched mugs gives hands something to do while the icing firms. Play music low enough that people lean in. The room should feel like a kitchen after supper, not a stage.

## Let the Cookies Tell the Evening

Do not worry too much about themes. A few cutters are enough: trees, bells, stars, mittens, moons. If you like a thread, choose one — a winter garden, a snowy village, ornaments for the tree — but let people wander from it. The plain cookie is not a failure; it is a beginning.

As the table fills, you will notice the little ceremonies forming. Someone saves the best sprinkles for last. Someone pipes every edge with surgical care. Someone makes a lopsided reindeer and names it. These are the keepsakes, though no one will say so at the time.

By the time the last sugar crystal is swept from the floor, the house will feel different — tender, bright, and faintly perfumed with vanilla. The cookies will be eaten, of course. But the memory of them will remain, sweet and patient, like candlelight on snow.
