A hand-knitted stocking isn't a decoration. It's a document. Every row is a decision someone made — this color, this stitch, this pattern — and when you hang it on the mantle, you're hanging hours of someone's attention in a world that rarely sits still long enough to count stitches.

## Choosing Your Wool

Start with a worsted-weight wool in a color that means something. Not just red because it's Christmas — maybe the deep cranberry your grandmother always wore, or a forest green that matches the tree you grew up decorating. The yarn is going to be around for decades if you do this right. Pick one that earns its place.

## The Fair Isle Tradition

Fair Isle knitting — the technique of carrying two colors across each row to create geometric patterns — is what gives traditional Christmas stockings their distinctive look. Snowflakes, reindeer, evergreen trees, all rendered in a grid of knit and purl that dates back to Scottish fishing communities.

The trick is tension. Carry the unused yarn loosely behind your work. Too tight and the fabric puckers; too loose and you get snagging loops on the inside. It takes about three inches of practice before your hands find the rhythm.

> The imperfections in a hand-knitted stocking aren't flaws. They're fingerprints.

## The Name on the Cuff

Embroider or knit the name directly into the cuff. Chain stitch in a contrasting yarn works beautifully and takes less than twenty minutes. This is the detail that transforms a stocking from something bought into something given.
